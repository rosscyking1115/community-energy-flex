# Product thesis

**The problem is not lack of data — it's translation.** People increasingly face
volatile prices, time-of-use tariffs, carbon-aware choices, EVs, heat pumps,
batteries and solar, but they do not know how to turn energy data into action.

**The product** recommends *when* to run flexible loads, given carbon intensity,
tariff, device constraints, comfort rules, and objective. It shows trade-offs
(cheapest ≠ greenest ≠ most comfortable), respects hard constraints, and makes
uncertainty visible.

## Principles

1. **Decision support, not automation** — it recommends; it does not control appliances.
2. **Show trade-offs** — and explain why the balanced option is what it is.
3. **User constraints come first** — a must-finish-by-07:00 task is never violated.
4. **No hidden optimisation** — every recommendation shows its assumptions.
5. **No energy-poverty harm** — help reduce cost; never shame or pressure.
6. **Privacy-first** — schedules reveal routines; aggregate and gate by role.
7. **Useful before smart meters** — manual task/tariff entry is enough to start.

## Who it's for

Households, shared homes, community centres, charities, small businesses, and
community energy groups. **Not** for grid operators, live balancing markets, or
safety-critical building control — it stays at the decision-support layer.

## What "good" looks like

A non-technical user understands what to do tomorrow and why; recommendations
respect their constraints; the tool is useful without a smart-meter feed; and a
community manager can see aggregate impact — all without ever claiming guaranteed
savings.
